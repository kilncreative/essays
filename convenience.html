ESSAY 07

Convenience is All You Need

APRIL 19, 2025

There exists a particular kind of melancholy in buildings constructed to outlast their creators, yet destined to be forgotten. I encountered such a structure last autumn—a brutalist pavilion standing at the edge of a man-made lake, its concrete form reflecting in still water. The architect, whose name has faded from public knowledge, had created a monument to permanence that paradoxically emphasized transience.

We are taught to understand architecture as a dialogue between form and function, between historical precedent and innovative vision. Yet there exists another dimension—the conversation between structure and time. The pavilion's sharp angles had softened over decades, its once-definitive edges now bearing the gentle erosion of countless rainstorms. The concrete, once uniform in color, had developed a patina of subtle variations—evidence of its ongoing negotiation with the elements.

The Japanese concept of wabi-sabi offers a framework for appreciating such unintended evolution. It celebrates the beauty found in imperfection and accepts the natural cycle of growth and decay. The building I encountered had not simply aged—it had matured, acquiring complexities unimaginable to its creator. Its surfaces held a record of time passing: water stains forming accidental compositions, cracks revealing the tension between human intention and natural forces.

Architecture exists in an unusual temporal state. Unlike other art forms that present themselves in their entirety—a painting viewed at once, a piece of music unfolding in a defined duration—buildings reveal themselves gradually through habitation and changing light. The pavilion appeared fundamentally different at dawn than at dusk, its character transforming with each hour. What seems a static medium becomes, upon extended observation, remarkably fluid.

Perhaps this quality explains our peculiar relationship with abandoned structures. They represent interrupted dialogues—conversations with space and time that have been suspended. The pavilion, no longer serving its original function as a gathering place, had become something else entirely: a framework for observing temporal processes, a lens through which to contemplate impermanence.

The modernist tradition from which brutalism emerged promised certainty through rational design. Yet what remains most striking about such buildings decades later is not their rationality but their capacity to accumulate meaning beyond their creators' intentions. The pavilion had become a repository for unplanned significance—a palimpsest where human design and natural processes had collaboratively authored something neither could have created alone.
